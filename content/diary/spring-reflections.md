Spring arrived quietly this year. One day the trees were bare; the next, there were tiny green buds everywhere. No announcement. No fanfare. Just slow, patient change that had been happening all along beneath the surface.

## The Illusion of Sudden Change

We tend to notice change only when it crosses a threshold of visibility. But the work — the real work — happened long before we saw it. The tree didn't start growing the day we noticed the buds. It had been preparing through the entire winter.

I think about this a lot in the context of learning. We study and practice and feel like nothing is happening. Then one day, something clicks, and it feels sudden. But it wasn't. It was accumulation.

## A Conversation with Time

There's a Chinese proverb I keep returning to:

> 种一棵树最好的时间是十年前，其次是现在。
> *The best time to plant a tree was ten years ago. The second best time is now.*

It's become almost cliché, but clichés become clichés because they're true. Every spring reminds me: start now. Be patient. Trust the process.

![Cherry blossoms](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1522383225653-ed111181a951?w=700&q=80)

## What I'm Starting This Spring

- Writing more consistently — even when I have nothing profound to say
- Reading one book per week (ambitious, but worth trying)
- Spending more time outdoors, away from screens

The buds don't worry about whether they'll become leaves. They just grow. Maybe there's wisdom in that.
